# The Public Data Lab — A Founding Philosophy

*The constitution of the studio. Every observatory begins here. When a decision is hard, this document — not taste, deadline, or novelty — is the tiebreaker.*

---

## Preamble

The world does not suffer from a shortage of public data. It suffers from a shortage of public understanding.

Satellites, sensors, censuses, and ministries produce more measurements of the planet and its people every day than any generation could read in a lifetime. Almost none of it changes what anyone believes or does. It sits in portals nobody returns to, in spreadsheets nobody opens twice, behind filters nobody knows how to set. The data is public in the narrow legal sense — released — and private in every sense that matters — unintelligible.

We exist to close that gap. Not to publish more data, but to convert data into understanding that a person can carry, act on, and trust.

This is the harder job, and it is the only one worth doing. Anyone can put a dataset online. We are building the institution that makes datasets *mean something* — reliably, beautifully, and for decades. The observatories we build are how the studio expresses itself. The studio — its standards, its judgment, its refusals — is the thing we are actually building.

---

## Who we serve, and who we serve first

We serve researchers, journalists, NGOs, policymakers, and curious citizens. These audiences want different depths of the same truth, and a great product does not choose between them — it *layers* for them.

But when their needs genuinely conflict, we resolve it in a fixed order:

1. **The truth** comes before every reader. We will disappoint all of them before we mislead any of them.
2. **The curious citizen** comes next. They have the least context, the least patience, and the most to gain. A product the newcomer can understand can always be given more depth; a product built only for the expert can never be made simple after the fact. Design for the newcomer's first minute and the expert's tenth hour — in that priority.
3. **The domain expert** is served through depth on demand, never at the surface. Their trust is earned by the rigor they find when they dig, not by the jargon they meet when they arrive.

We do not serve ourselves. Not our cleverness, not our aesthetic vanity, not our engagement metrics. A product that exists to flatter its makers is a failure no matter how many people visit it.

---

## Why most public data products fail

We study failure because we intend to avoid it, deliberately and by name.

**Dashboards are abandoned because they answer no question.** A dashboard hands the reader a control panel and says *you figure it out*. But people do not arrive with a query; they arrive with a question — "Is my city's air getting worse?" — and the burden of turning that question into filter settings is exactly the labor they came to have done for them. A tool that requires you to already know the answer to configure it will be visited once.

**Government dashboards are hard to use because they are organized around the institution, not the question.** They mirror the org chart: this bureau's data here, that department's there. They confuse *transparency* (we released it) with *accountability* (we made it legible). And in their fear of ever being wrong, they bury every figure under every caveat and every option, until paralysis masquerades as rigor.

**People trust Our World in Data because it shows its work and still takes a position.** The source is one click away. The method is stated. The chart has a point of view — and shows you the data that could undermine it. It treats you as intelligent without assuming you are already expert. That combination — conviction plus receipts — is the entire basis of trust, and it is rarer than it should be.

The lesson is not "add more." It is that comprehension is a design achievement, not a data-volume achievement. More data, more options, and more caveats usually subtract understanding. Our craft is the discipline of subtraction in service of truth.

## What we believe

These are the principles. They are numbered so they can be cited. When we say a design "violates Principle 4," everyone should know what that means.

### 1. Start with a question, not a dataset.
Every product answers a real question a real person actually has, stated in one sentence they would recognize as their own. If we cannot write that sentence, we do not yet have a product — we have data looking for a purpose. The dataset is the means. The question is the product.

### 2. The default view must already answer something.
No blank slates. No "select parameters to begin." No empty chart waiting to be configured. The moment a reader arrives, the product should teach them something true and important — the headline answer, at the largest scale, with the clearest framing. Depth is offered *on demand*, never *on arrival*. Progressive disclosure is not a nicety; it is the core mechanic of understanding.

### 3. Show your work, always, in one action.
Every number must trace to its source in a single, obvious action. Every method must be stateable in plain language. Trust is not asserted through authority ("we are the experts"); it is earned through transparency of method ("here is exactly how we know this, check us"). A figure whose provenance we cannot show is a figure we do not publish.

### 4. Uncertainty is information, not weakness.
We never launder an estimate into a fact. We distinguish, visibly and always, what is **measured**, what is **estimated**, what is **modeled**, and what is **projected** — because a reader who cannot tell them apart has been misled, however accurate our numbers. The goal is not maximum confidence; it is *calibrated* confidence. A well-communicated "we don't know precisely, and here is the range" is a triumph, not an embarrassment.

### 5. Rigor and simplicity are the same discipline, not opposing ones.
The reader's simplicity is bought with our complexity. We do the hard analytical work — the cleaning, the reconciliation, the uncertainty quantification — precisely so the reader does not have to. Simplicity earned by *absorbing* complexity is our craft. Simplicity achieved by *hiding* complexity is a lie, and we can always tell the difference by asking one question: if the reader dug all the way down, would they feel informed or deceived?

### 6. Design is an argument about what matters.
There is no neutral chart. Every default, every ordering, every color, every choice of scale and framing is an editorial act that tells the reader what to care about. Neutrality is a fiction that lets makers avoid responsibility for choices they are making anyway. We reject it. We make our editorial choices deliberately, own them openly, and defend them on the merits — *and we never distort the data to win the argument.* Persuasion by clarity is our method; persuasion by distortion is our disgrace.

### 7. Beauty is a functional property.
Craft is not decoration. Consistency, typography, restraint, and visual care are how a product signals that its makers were careful — and a reader rightly infers that people careless with a color scale may have been careless with a number. Aesthetic excellence is therefore an epistemic argument: *we sweated the details you can see, so trust us with the ones you can't.* But beauty is strictly subordinate to honesty. A beautiful chart that misleads is worse than an ugly one that informs, because it misleads *persuasively*. No chartjunk, no decoration that competes with meaning, no beauty bought with a distorted axis.

### 8. Comparability is a feature; false equivalence is a sin.
Standardization across observatories is how the studio compounds — shared scales, shared vocabulary, shared chart grammar let a reader move from the Water Observatory to the Air Quality Explorer without relearning how to read. So we standardize relentlessly. But we never force two things into the same frame when the underlying realities differ. A clean comparison of things that are not actually comparable is one of the most elegant ways to lie, and we will not do it however tempting the visual symmetry.

### 9. Build for the decade, not the launch.
Data updates. Links rot. Methods improve. Institutions rename themselves. A product that is correct on launch day and quietly wrong a year later has betrayed everyone who trusted it in between. So we date every figure, version every method, cite the vintage of every source, and design for the update as a first-class event rather than an afterthought. The question is never "does it work today?" It is "will it still be trustworthy, and maintainable, in five years?"

### 10. Do no harm with data.
Public data can stigmatize a neighborhood, identify an individual through small numbers, or be weaponized against the very people it describes. Aggregation, framing, and release are ethical acts, not merely technical ones. Before we publish, we ask who is helped and who is harmed by a number and its framing — and when a truthful presentation would cause disproportionate harm, we change the presentation, not the truth. We are accountable for how our work is *used*, not only for whether it is *accurate*.

### 11. The studio is the product.
No single observatory is the point. What compounds — what is still valuable in ten years — is the standard, the method, the shared vocabulary, and the earned reputation. Every project pays into that common well and draws from it. We would rather ship one observatory that raises the whole studio's standard than five that dilute it. Sameness in service of quality is a feature; we guard only against sameness that becomes soulless template-stamping, and against its opposite — every project vainly reinventing what the studio already knows.

## The tensions we resolve

A constitution earns its keep not by listing virtues but by saying which virtue wins when two collide. These are our standing resolutions.

- **Rigor vs. accessibility →** resolved by *layering*. We never trade one for the other; we stack them. Simple surface, rigorous depths, a continuous path between.
- **Completeness vs. clarity →** clarity wins at the surface, completeness is available beneath it. Showing everything is how you communicate nothing. Editorial selection is not a failure of thoroughness; it *is* the thoroughness.
- **A point of view vs. objectivity →** resolved by *transparent subjectivity*. We take positions and show the evidence and choices behind them, so a reader can disagree with our judgment while trusting our facts.
- **Speed vs. trust →** trust wins, every time. We would rather ship later than ship wrong. Reputation is decades in the earning and one bad number in the losing.
- **Beauty vs. honesty →** honesty wins, and we reject the premise that they usually conflict. The rare times they truly do, the distortion goes, however lovely.

Write these down because under deadline pressure everyone forgets them, and the pressure is exactly when they matter.

## On being "AI-first"

We will use artificial intelligence heavily — to find data, reconcile sources, draft explanations, translate, summarize, and scale our output far beyond what our headcount would otherwise allow. This is a genuine advantage and we intend to press it.

But "AI-first" describes our *method*, never our *standard of truth*, and the two must never be confused. A model is a tool for producing more, faster. It is not a source of authority, and it is never an excuse to lower the bar. Three commitments are non-negotiable:

- **No unaccountable claims.** Nothing a model generates reaches a reader as fact without the same provenance we demand of ourselves (Principle 3). The reader should never have to trust our model. They should always be able to check our source.
- **Judgment stays human.** AI drafts; people decide. The editorial choices of Principle 6 — what matters, what to foreground, what a number means — are ours to own and defend. We do not launder responsibility through automation.
- **Scale multiplies the standard, it does not replace it.** AI lets us hold the same rigor across ten observatories that we once could barely hold across one. If it is ever producing more at a lower standard, we are using it exactly backwards.

The reader must never be able to tell which sentence a machine drafted — not because we hid it, but because every sentence cleared the same bar.

## What we refuse to build

Naming our refusals is how we keep our values from eroding one reasonable-sounding exception at a time.

- **Dashboards that answer no question** — control panels that offload the analytical work onto the reader (Principle 1, 2).
- **Data dumps dressed as transparency** — releasing everything and calling the reader's resulting confusion their own fault (Principle 2).
- **Confidence theater** — single numbers with the uncertainty stripped off to look authoritative (Principle 4).
- **Persuasion by distortion** — truncated axes, cherry-picked ranges, false equivalences, or any beauty bought with a lie (Principles 6, 7, 8).
- **Engagement traps** — dark patterns, vanity metrics, or designs that optimize for time-on-site over understanding-per-minute. We measure whether people *understood*, not whether they *stayed*.
- **Orphan products** — anything we are not prepared to keep correct as its data changes (Principle 9).

If a proposed product resembles anything on this list, the burden is on the proposer to prove it is not — not on the studio to tolerate it.

---

## What "done" means

A product is finished — not shipped, *finished* — only when all of the following are true. This is the standard the whole studio is accountable to.

- A first-time reader understands the central point within the first minute, without instruction.
- Every figure traces to a documented source in one action, and its vintage is visible.
- Measured, estimated, modeled, and projected values are unmistakably distinguished.
- A domain expert who digs to the bottom finds rigor, not embarrassment.
- Nothing on screen misleads, even at a glance, even to someone who reads only the headline and the chart shape.
- It is beautiful — and its beauty carries meaning rather than competing with it.
- It will survive its next data update, and we know exactly how that update will happen.

"Good enough to ship" and "meets the standard" are different sentences. We only permit ourselves the second.

---

## The test

When you are deep in a decision and the principles seem to point in different directions, return to the one question this entire document exists to serve:

**Does this help a real person understand something true that matters to them — and could they check us if they doubted us?**

If yes, we are doing our job. If no, nothing else we got right will save it.

We are not in the business of publishing data. We are in the business of earning understanding and deserving trust — one honest, beautiful, rigorous answer at a time, for years longer than anyone expects us to bother.

That is the studio. Everything else is an observatory.
